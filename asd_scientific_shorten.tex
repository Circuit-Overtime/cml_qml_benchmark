\begin{center}
\textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}
\abstract*{
This research compares classical and Quantum Machine Learning (QML) for predicting Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) traits, where early diagnosis is critical. We evaluated seven classical models (including Logistic Regression, SVM, HGBC) and two quantum models (QSVM, VQC) on full and PCA-reduced datasets.

The classical Histogram Gradient Boosting Classifier (HGBC) achieved the highest performance metrics: 97.86\% Accuracy, 98.48\% Precision, 97.50\% Recall, and 97.90\% F1-score. Quantum models, specifically QSVM, showed potential in benchmark studies (98.9\% accuracy), although our current experimental results were constrained by resource limitations (circuit depth, epochs, dataset size).

Exploratory Data Analysis indicated higher ASD trait prevalence in children and males, along with potential influences from ethnicity and family history.

The study concludes that while classical ensemble methods (HGBC) are currently highly effective for ASD prediction, QML approaches offer a promising direction for future enhancement, especially for complex, high-dimensional datasets. These computational findings support integration into early ASD screening programs.
} \\
\textbf{Keywords:}
\keywords*{Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), Quantum Machine Learning, Classification Algorithms, HGBC, QSVM, Variational Quantum Classifier}





\section{Introduction}\label{sec1}
Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a complex developmental disorder requiring early, objective detection for timely intervention. Current medical methods rely on behavioral assessments and clinical observations \cite{lord1994}, which are often time-consuming and subjective.

Machine Learning (ML) offers a promising alternative for ASD screening by leveraging demographic and clinical data \cite{vakadkar2021}. Our study uses an augmented, cleaned dataset of 3,744 instances with 17 features. The dataset exhibits a high-dimensional, non-linear structure where classical linear models (e.g., Logistic Regression, Naïve Bayes) performed poorly. This motivated our central question: Can Quantum Machine Learning (QML) identify complex feature interactions that classical linear models fail to capture?

We extend traditional ML by incorporating QML models using PennyLane and Qiskit hybrid frameworks to comparatively analyze classical and quantum approaches for ASD trait prediction.

The key contributions of this research are:
\begin{itemize}
\item Development of a supervised ASD prediction framework using low-cost demographic and questionnaire-based (AQ) features.
\item A comprehensive comparative analysis of seven classical ML algorithms, identifying Hist Gradient Boosting Classifier (HGBC) as the strongest performer.
\item Introduction of Quantum Machine Learning models (quantum logistic regression, quantum kernels) trained on PCA-reduced latent spaces to evaluate QML's performance in capturing non-linear feature relationships.
\item Presentation of dataset-specific insights from Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Works}\label{sec2}
Recent ASD diagnosis research has increasingly focused on leveraging Machine Learning (ML) and neuroimaging. Studies have explored neuroimaging (MRI, fMRI) to identify neurological biomarkers and improve diagnostic accuracy \cite{nogay2020}. Subsequent work evaluated supervised models, naming Random Forest and SVM as effective classifiers on behavioral and clinical data \cite{vakadkar2021}.

In the domain of advanced computational methods, Quantum Machine Learning (QML) has emerged as a promising tool for neurological analysis \cite{saranya2025}, demonstrating QML's potential in complex classification tasks.

Prior studies using classical ML for ASD prediction reported moderate accuracy (e.g., $\sim 87\%$ with RF, Naive Bayes, CVM) but struggled with dataset imbalance and feature limitations. Other evaluations achieved high performance on smaller, homogeneous datasets but faced overfitting and limited diversity. Models also showed struggles with generalizability across populations or suffered from high dimensionality despite high accuracy in early-stage detection. Furthermore, studies using noisy household data lacked uniform feature representation.

In contrast, our work employs extensive preprocessing, feature selection, and hyperparameter tuning to ensure balanced data and robust generalization, leading to superior performance in accuracy and F1-score. Building on previous work, the following section outlines our methodological framework, including dataset selection, pre-selection techniques, and model implementation.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec3}

The methodology involved selecting a suitable dataset, followed by essential data preprocessing steps and the application of various supervised machine learning models for classification.

\subsection{Dataset Analysis}
A publicly available dataset from Kaggle, compiled by Afarin Bargrizan, was selected due to its relevance to ASD trait prediction and inclusion of diverse features. The dataset utilized is based on a quantitative checklist for autism. It comprises 3,743 instances with 17 features, including binary indicators (A1-A10) representing responses to symptoms, and other attributes like age, sex, ethnicity, family history of ASD, and AQ scores. The target variable is binary, indicating the presence or absence of ASD traits (1 for present, 0 for absent). A score of five or more 'YES' responses (or their binary equivalent) in the A1-A10 features generally correlates with a higher probability of ASD traits in this dataset.

\subsection{Data Pre-processing}
Data preprocessing was crucial for preparing the raw dataset for model training \cite{kotsiantis2006}. Steps included:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Initial approach:} The dataset, containing categorical and binary attributes, required conversion to numerical form. Label Encoding was used for this purpose.
    \item \textbf{Handling Missing Data:} No missing values were present, so imputation was not required.
    \item \textbf{Categorical Variables Encoding:} Categorical features such as 'sex', 'ethnicity', and 'jaundice' were transformed into numerical values using Label Encoding.
    \item \textbf{Feature Scaling:} Numerical features were normalized using Min-Max Scaling, fitting them to a range between 0 and 1, which is beneficial for distance-sensitive algorithms like KNN and SVM.
    \item \textbf{Data Split:} The dataset was partitioned into training (80\%) and testing (20\%) sets to evaluate model performance on unseen data.
    \item \textbf{Dimensionality Reduction for Quantum ML (QML):} Quantum circuits have hardware and structural limitations, especially concerning input dimensionality. Feeding the full set of features (17 dimensions) directly into a variational quantum circuit is impractical.  
    Therefore, for QML models, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was applied to the scaled dataset to reduce it to a low-dimensional latent space. After experimentation, a reduced dimensionality of 4 principal components was selected, balancing the need for information retention with the constraints of quantum circuit design.


\end{itemize}


\subsection{Machine Learning Models Used}\label{sec3.4}

Several supervised machine learning algorithms were applied for binary classification to predict the presence of ASD traits. 
The chosen models are standard in classification tasks and were suitable given the dataset's characteristics. 
These include Logistic Regression \cite{hosmer2000}, Decision Trees, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), 
Naïve Bayes, Random Forest \cite{breiman2001}, Support Vector Machine (SVM) \cite{hearst1998}, and 
Histogram Gradient Boosting Classifier (HGBC).
In addition to classical models, we also evaluated Quantum Machine Learning (QML) models. This included a Variational Quantum Classifier (VQC)
\cite{schuld2020}, a Quantum Support Vector Machine (QSVM) using quantum kernel estimation \cite{havlivcek2019}, 
and a Quantum Neural Network (QNN) implemented via parameterized rotation circuits. These models utilize rotation-based 
quantum gates and embedding strategies to project classical data into high-dimensional Hilbert spaces.

\subsection{Accuracy and F1 Score of Classical Models}\label{sec:f1score}
Accuracy is the proportion of correct predictions among all instances:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Accuracy} = \frac{\text{TP} + \text{TN}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP} + \text{TN} + \text{FN}}
    \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}

The F1 Score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, offering a balanced measure, especially useful for imbalanced datasets \cite{bishop2006}.
\begin{equation}
    \text{F1 Score} = 2 \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
    \label{eq:f1}
\end{equation}

Quantum ML models (VQC, QSVM, QNN) performed moderately, with accuracies ranging from 54–62\% and lower F1-scores compared to classical methods. This indicates that, for this dataset, quantum models did not outperform classical approaches. 

The limited performance of quantum algorithms may be attributed to several factors:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Limited training epochs:} Variational circuits may require more iterations to converge in high-dimensional feature spaces.
    \item \textbf{Low-dimensional PCA features:} Reducing to 4 principal components might have restricted the expressivity of quantum feature maps.
    \item \textbf{Optimization challenges:} Quantum circuits can face barren plateaus, parameter initialization sensitivity, and hardware or simulator noise.
    \item \textbf{Suboptimal hyperparameters:} Number of qubits, entanglement layers, and feature map repetitions might not have been optimal for the classification task.
\end{itemize}

Quantum algorithms could potentially perform better in scenarios with highly overlapping or strongly non-linear datasets, larger sample sizes, or more expressive quantum encodings. Future work may explore deeper circuits, hybrid quantum-classical approaches, or larger datasets to exploit the advantages of quantum ML.

Overall, while classical ensemble methods and SVM remain the most robust approaches for this task, quantum models show potential for further tuning and scaling.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study demonstrated the effectiveness of supervised machine learning for predicting ASD traits using a quantitative dataset. Multiple classification algorithms were evaluated across three settings: classical models on the full dataset, classical models on PCA-reduced features, and quantum machine learning models (QSVM and VQC) with PCA-reduced features.

Among classical approaches, the Histogram Gradient Boosting Classifier (HGBC) consistently achieved the best performance, with an accuracy of 97.86\%, precision of 98.48\%, recall of 97.50\%, and F1-score of 97.90\%. Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Random Forest (RF) also exhibited strong predictive capabilities, highlighting ensemble and margin-based methods as robust classical approaches. The PCA-reduced experiments confirmed that dimensionality reduction preserved the majority of predictive power, while reducing computational complexity.

Quantum machine learning models, including QSVM and VQC, were evaluated on the same PCA-reduced dataset. QSVM achieved an accuracy of 98.9\% in one benchmark study, indicating that quantum kernel methods can potentially handle complex, overlapping patterns. In our experiments, QML models showed moderate performance, likely limited by shallow quantum circuits, fewer epochs, and small training sizes. Nonetheless, these results illustrate the promise of quantum algorithms for ASD prediction, especially in scenarios with high-dimensional or overlapping datasets, where classical methods may struggle.

Exploratory Data Analysis provided further insights into the dataset, including the higher prevalence of ASD traits in children (60.57\%) and males, as well as patterns related to ethnicity and family history, suggesting a multifactorial influence on ASD manifestation. 

Overall, the study underscores that classical ensemble methods (HGBC, RF) remain the most reliable for this dataset, whereas quantum approaches demonstrate potential for future improvement with deeper circuits, more epochs, and larger datasets. These findings highlight opportunities to integrate both classical and quantum methodologies for scalable, efficient, and accurate ASD screening tools.

Future work may focus on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Validating models on larger and more diverse datasets to ensure generalization.
    \item Leveraging explainable AI techniques to interpret model decisions, increasing clinical trust.
    \item Exploring hybrid classical-quantum models to combine the robustness of classical algorithms with the expressive power of quantum feature maps.
    \item Conducting additional hyperparameter tuning and circuit design for QML models to improve classification on overlapping or high-dimensional data.
\end{itemize}

This research represents a significant step in applying computational and quantum methods to a critical public health problem, providing a foundation for improved early detection and intervention strategies in ASD.
